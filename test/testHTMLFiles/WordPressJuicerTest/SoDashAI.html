How does the SoDash AI work?

Posted on August 5, 2011 by winterstein

The SoDash AI learns from the user. It looks for patterns in what the user is doing, specifically in the tags they apply. By following the user’s actions, the AI learns to recognise relevant patterns with increasing confidence.

The AI considers both who is talking and what they say. Regarding the person, it looks at biographical information, your relationship with them, and their previous messages. Regarding the message, it learns patterns involving both keywords and phrases (e.g. that “shit hot” does not mean the same as “shit”).


3D text map of Manhattan from online sources (cc)PushAndPlay@Flickr

SoDash draws on a general knowledge of language and sentiment. But it adapts its understanding to fit the project. E.g. “Coke is better than Pepsi” has a very different meaning to you depending on whether you are CocaCola or PepsiCo. There is no such thing as generic analysis, just as there is no such thing as a generic project or customer.

Using computers to analyse human text is known as Natural Language Processing (NLP). Algorithms – mathematical recipes – pick apart the text looking for patterns. SoDash’s learning mechanism uses a proprietary social text classification algorithm which draws on research in linguistics and advanced statistics.

The SoDash algorithm is a more advanced version of the algorithms used in spam filters. For those familiar with statistical AI, our method is a generative Bayesian model related to Markov modelling techniques. However we’ve developed an improved mathematical model which goes beyond these techniques. We’ve added in analysis of the person talking and their relationship to you — plus faster learning and better phrase handling.

We are benchmarking against algorithms from a leading AI/search company. Preliminary results show SoDash performs considerably better.

Classification performance can be characterised in terms of precision (what proportion of tagged messages are tagged correctly) and recall (what proportion of messages that should be tagged are tagged). Precision and recall can only be established relative to particular data sets, but SoDash performs extremely well on both metrics, typically equalling or exceeding baselines for human performance once it has been sufficiently trained.

SoDash is currently optimised for English: it knows about English morphology (the structure of words; e.g. that “run” and “running” are related), similar words, and can pick out the significant words in a sentence. However the core algorithm is language agnostic, and new language modules can easily be added. The mathematics at the heart of the system are universal.
